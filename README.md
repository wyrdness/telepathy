## Overview

Telepathy is the purported transmission of information — thoughts, feelings, mental images, or sensations — from one person's mind directly to another's, without using any known channel of human sensory perception. The term was coined by the classical scholar and Society for Psychical Research (SPR) co-founder Frederic W. H. Myers in 1882, from Ancient Greek τῆλε ('distant') and πάθος ('feeling, perception'). Telepathy has been the central subject of parapsychological research from the SPR's 'Phantasms of the Living' (1886) through J. B. Rhine's Zener-card experiments at Duke University in the 1930s, the Maimonides dream-telepathy studies of the 1960s–70s, and the ganzfeld experiments of the 1970s–2000s. Mainstream scientific reviews — including the 1988 U.S. National Research Council report and successive critiques by Ray Hyman, Susan Blackmore, and others — have concluded that no replicable evidence demonstrates telepathy and that the parapsychological literature is best explained by methodological flaws, sensory leakage, statistical errors, and (in some cases) outright fraud. Telepathy is therefore typically classified as a pseudoscience, while remaining a powerful and widely held cultural belief.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Psi field / nonlocal mind** _(Dean Radin, Stephan Schwartz)_

Some parapsychologists hypothesise a 'psi' faculty that transfers information nonlocally, possibly drawing analogies to quantum entanglement.

### Scientific

**No evidence beyond chance** _(Ray Hyman, Susan Blackmore, James Alcock, Daniel Druckman & Robert Swets (NRC, 1988))_

Mainstream scientific consensus, supported by the 1988 NRC report and successive critical reviews, is that the experimental literature does not establish telepathy beyond chance and is best explained by methodological errors and selection effects.

### Skeptical

**Cold reading and sensory leakage** _(Joseph Banks Rhine (in later self-criticism), Persi Diaconis, James Randi)_

Documented cases of stage 'mind readers' (Bishop, Cumberland) and laboratory experiments (Creery sisters, Soal) have been shown to depend on muscle reading, signalling, or recording errors.

**Cognitive biases** _(Susan Blackmore, Christopher French)_

Confirmation bias, selective memory, and the 'illusion of agency' explain anecdotal beliefs in personal telepathic experiences without invoking psi.
